EXPLORING THE FINE LINE BETWEEN SCOPE AND MEANING OF SECTIONS 9(1)(a) & 9(1)(b) OF THE TRADE MARKS ACT, 1999?

A & B law Venture




Basis Section 9(1)(a)- Distinctiveness Section 9(1)(b)- Descriptiveness
Definition Prohibits registration of marks that lack any distinctive character. Prohibits registration of marks that are merely descriptive of the goods or services.
Nature of Marks EXPLORING THE FINE LINE BETWEEN SCOPE AND MEANING OF SECTIONS 9(1)(a) & 9(1)(b) OF THE TRADE MARKS ACT, 1999 Basis Section 9(1)(a)- Distinctiveness Section 9(1)(b)- Descriptiveness Definition Prohibits registration of marks that lack any distinctive character. Prohibits registration of marks that are merely descriptive of the goods or services. Nature of Marks Marks that are incapable of distinguishing the goods or services of one person from those of others. Marks that directly describe characteristics such as kind, quality, quantity, intended purpose, or value of the goods/services.
Examples Generic terms like "Computer" for computer products or "Food" for food items. Descriptive terms like "Fresh" for food products or "Best Quality" for a brand of goods
Acquired Distinctiveness Does not allow for acquired distinctiveness; marks must inherently possess distinctiveness to be registered Allows for acquired distinctiveness; a descriptive mark can be registered if it has gained a secondary meaning through extensive use.
Legal Precedent In the case of Hindustan Unilever Limited v. The Registrar of Trademarks, marks like "GLOW & LOVELY" were rejected due to lack of distinctiveness. A descriptive mark may be registered if it has lost its primary meaning and is recognized by consumers as a source identifier, as seen in various trademark cases.
Purpose To prevent monopolization of non-distinctive terms that do not serve to identify a single source. To ensure that descriptive terms remain available for public use and cannot be monopolized by one entity
